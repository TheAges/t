Initial research and concept creation

The project began with the handover of a comprehensive scenario from another team, which included system maps, user journeys, and a detailed analysis of user needs for shared autonomous vehicle services. This foundational research provided us with a clear understanding of the specific challenges faced by passengers in autonomous vehicles, particularly in a shared, work-oriented setting. The user personas defined by the previous workgroup were primarily professionals who needed to remain productive while traveling within the city. These individuals required seamless access to information, tools, and entertainment in a non-disruptive manner as they moved through urban environments.

With these user needs clearly defined, our focus turned to addressing how passengers would interact with the vehicle and access information while on the move. Traditional car interiors no longer applied to this new context, where passengers werenâ€™t driving but rather using the travel time to work, collaborate, or stay informed. We needed to design a system that would accommodate this shift, offering an efficient and engaging user experience.

This leaded us to envisioned a forward-looking interior car concept tailored for tomorrow's shared autonomous vehicle services. Among the most important features, the concept must leverage 5G tech, which offers the high-speed connectivity needed for seamless data transfer and cloud-based services, making the vehicle an extension of their workspace. Then, we decided to employ a windshield display, which would allow the carâ€™s windshield to double as an infotainment and information hub. This display could provide passengers with real-time information about the outside environment, notifications, and collaborative work tools, all while maintaining a minimalist, non-intrusive interface that keeps the focus on both the work and the journey. Pared with the windshield, gesture-based interfaces and conversational AI appeared to be a clear match, which would allow users to interact with the system in a natural, intuitive way. These interfaces would enable passengers to interact with the windshield display through voice and simple gestures, offering an ergonomic way to manage tasks while remaining comfortably seated during their journey. By integrating haptic feedback into the system, passengers would be able to feel their interactions in the air, receiving tactile responses to their gestures. This would provide a more tangible and intuitive experience, making it easier to interact with information while minimizing distraction from the work at hand.
